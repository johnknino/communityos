# CommUnity OS: A Community Health Intervention Operating Through Civic Infrastructure

**Robert Wood Johnson Foundation — Grant Narrative Draft**
March 2026

---

## The Problem

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act enacted the largest cuts to American safety net programs in history: $1 trillion from Medicaid, up to $187 billion from SNAP. Over 8,000 federal web pages and 3,000 datasets have been removed or altered. The USDA eliminated its own 30-year hunger survey.

These cuts land hardest on communities that already carry the heaviest health burden. In Chicago's 60608 zip code (Pilsen), 82% of residents are Hispanic. Median household income is $42,000. The area has higher rates of diabetes, hypertension, and obesity than the city average. When the safety net contracts, these residents lose not just food and health coverage — they lose the information systems that connect them to what remains.

No platform bridges the gap from crisis survival to community governance. Existing civic technology occupies segments: Findhelp.org addresses service navigation. Code for America addresses government access. Decidim addresses participatory budgeting. None connect them. None articulate the theory that the connection matters — that a person who finds food through a platform might, over time, use that same platform to check on their elected official, assess a community program, and propose policy changes.

The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation's County Health Rankings demonstrate that 40% of health outcomes are driven by social and economic factors — the very factors that civic participation can influence. Kawachi and Berkman (2000) established that community-level social capital independently predicts health outcomes. Kim and Kawachi (2007) showed that even after controlling for individual factors, communities with higher civic participation have better self-rated health.

The missing link is infrastructure: a free, bilingual, community-owned platform that connects health information to civic action, measures the connection, and costs nothing to sustain.

## The Intervention

CommUnity OS is a free bilingual civic resilience platform that organizes community life along a five-stage arc: Survive (food, health, emergencies), Understand (rights, money, law), Connect (neighbors, mutual aid), Grow (food sovereignty, gardens), and Govern (evaluate leaders, audit institutions, propose policy). The platform operates on a single reasoning scaffold derived from clinical diagnostic medicine: Normal → Broken → Fix. How does this system work? Where is it failing? What can you do about it?

The platform is not a website. It is a community nervous system. One person enters a zip code. That is the seed. From that single input, they see what's available near them, what they need to know, who's nearby, and how their community is doing. When a second person enters the same zip, a community forms. When communities connect through the federated data chain, a national picture emerges — bottom-up, for the first time.

**Key features as health intervention:**

The platform delivers clinical-grade health information at 6th grade reading level. A camera-based heart rate monitor uses the phone's camera to detect pulse. Blood pressure, blood sugar, temperature, and BMI interpreters provide plain-language N→B→F explanations: what's normal, what your number means, what to do. Twenty-seven health and financial literacy guides cover the topics that drive the social determinants: prescription access, insurance, diabetes, blood pressure, antibiotics, food safety, emergency preparedness, tenant rights, immigration rights, disability services.

The Community Health Pulse aggregates anonymous vitals contributions with k≥11 suppression, allowing a neighborhood to see its own health patterns — the percentage with elevated blood pressure, the average fasting glucose, the proportion in the prediabetic range — without any individual being identifiable. This is community-level health surveillance owned and operated by the community itself.

The governance tools — leader check, community assessment, institutional audit, and structured deliberation — operate through the same N→B→F scaffold. A resident evaluating their alderman uses the same diagnostic reasoning as a patient understanding their blood pressure. The scaffold transfers because the structure of the problem transfers: every system has normal function, failure modes, and corrective actions.

**The theoretical claim:** Civic participation is a health intervention because it operates through the same social determinants that drive 40% of health outcomes. The platform delivers health information AND develops civic capacity, and the research says both improve health outcomes. The dual function is not accidental — it is architectural.

## The Evidence Base

The intervention draws on six established research domains:

**Health literacy (Kutner et al. 2006; Doak, Doak & Root):** Only 12% of U.S. adults have proficient health literacy. Patients who understand health materials are 50% more likely to follow medical advice. All platform content is written at SMOG grade 7.1 or below, exceeding NIH and AMA recommendations.

**Civic participation and health (Kawachi & Berkman 2000; Kim & Kawachi 2007):** Community-level social capital — trust, reciprocity, civic engagement — independently predicts health outcomes. The platform builds social capital by connecting neighbors around shared needs.

**Deliberative democracy (Fishkin 2009, 2018):** Structured deliberation with balanced briefing materials reduces polarization, increases evidence-based reasoning, and produces decisions that participants commit to. The platform's proposal tool implements Fishkin's five criteria for legitimate deliberation.

**Ladder of participation (Arnstein 1969; Gaventa 2006):** The platform's five-stage arc maps directly to Arnstein's ladder, crossing the critical threshold from tokenism to citizen power between the Understand and Connect stages.

**Community data governance (CARE Principles; OCAP; Ada Lovelace Institute 2021):** The platform's data governance charter ensures communities own their data, control what is shared, and can revoke access in one click.

**Diagnostic reasoning transfer (Schön 1983; Geva-May 2005):** The N→B→F scaffold derives from clinical medicine and transfers to civic domains because the structure of the problem — normal function, failure mode, corrective action — is domain-agnostic.

## Study Design

**Primary research question:** Does engagement with a bilingual civic resilience platform produce measurable upward movement on Arnstein's Ladder of Citizen Participation, from information access (Survive/Understand) to citizen power (Connect/Grow/Govern)?

**Secondary questions:**
1. Does health literacy improve after repeated exposure to N→B→F health interpretations?
2. Does civic participation correlate with self-rated health improvement?
3. Does the platform's federated architecture produce actionable community-level health data?

**Methods:** Anonymous page-flow tracking (which pages are visited after which) measures arc movement without collecting PII. Pre/post health literacy assessment using validated instruments (REALM-SF, NVS). Community Health Pulse data provides population-level health metrics. All data collection follows the platform's k≥11 suppression and Safe Harbor de-identification standards.

**Population:** Residents of Chicago zip codes 60608 (Pilsen), 60623 (Lawndale), and 60632 (Brighton Park) — majority Hispanic communities with high rates of chronic disease and low rates of civic participation.

**Timeline:** 18 months. Months 1-3: community onboarding and seed content. Months 4-12: data collection and platform iteration. Months 13-18: analysis and publication.

## Budget Justification

The platform's operating cost is $7/year (domain registration). Hosting is free (Netlify/Cloudflare Pages). Backend is free (Google Apps Script + Google Sheets). Database cache is free (Firebase free tier). The intervention requires no technology procurement, no server infrastructure, and no ongoing licensing fees.

Grant funds would support: community engagement coordinator (bilingual, 0.5 FTE), research assistant (data analysis, 0.25 FTE), community Steward stipends ($500/community/year × 3 communities), travel and community event costs, and publication/dissemination.

**Requested amount:** $150,000 over 18 months.

## Significance

No existing platform connects health information delivery to civic governance infrastructure. No existing study measures whether that connection produces health outcomes. CommUnity OS is both the intervention and the instrument — it delivers the content, measures the engagement, and generates the community-level data needed to answer the question.

If the arc is real — if people who find food through a platform eventually check on their alderman — then civic infrastructure is a health intervention. That finding would reshape how foundations, health departments, and community organizations invest in community health. Not more clinics. Not more programs. Infrastructure. $7 a year. A zip code. The memories returned to the community.
