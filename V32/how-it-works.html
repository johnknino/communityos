How This Works

No server. No database. No accounts. No tracking. Here's why.

Why This Exists

A parent on the southwest side of Chicago looked at the systems his family depends on — schools, clinics, city services, elected officials — and asked three questions: How is this supposed to work? Where is it failing? What can we do about it?

The programs exist. $60 billion in benefits goes unclaimed every year. Half of medical bills have errors. 92% of legal problems get no legal help. Every zip code in America has elected officials, but most people can't name them. The services are there. The access isn't.

So a Concerned Parent built the access layer. Not a startup. Not a grant project. Infrastructure — the kind that costs $7 a year and can't be shut down, bought out, or turned into an advertising platform. The kind any neighborhood can run without asking permission.

The platform now watches its own community's health, diagnoses where civic systems are failing, cross-references community data with federal health and economic indicators, and adapts. It applies the same diagnostic reasoning a doctor uses with a patient — except the patient is a neighborhood, and the community owns the diagnosis.

What This Means

Throughout history, civic infrastructure has required institutional power. Roads required empires. Courts required states. Public schools required governments. Libraries required philanthropy. Every system that connects citizens to governance has required concentrated money and concentrated authority to build and sustain.

The internet changed the cost of communication — but not the cost of civic infrastructure. Platforms that connect neighbors cost hundreds of millions to build and operate. When the funding runs out, they die. Over 70 civic technology projects have failed this way, including organizations backed by $695 million in sector investment.

What changed is the technology stack. Free global content delivery networks, serverless computing, open government data, and browser-native tools converged in the last five years. For the first time, it is possible to build nationally scalable civic infrastructure without institutional backing, without ongoing funding, and without anyone's permission. This platform is the proof. The question is no longer whether communities can afford civic infrastructure. The question is what they build with it.

The Architecture

CommUnity OS has two layers, and they work differently on purpose.

Layer 1: Survival tools. The benefit screener, dispute letter generator, health calculators, drug price lookup, court navigator, and all 54 knowledge guides are static HTML files hosted on a free content delivery network. There is no server processing your requests. Everything runs in your browser, on your device. The calculations happen on your phone. The letters are generated on your phone. Nothing leaves your phone. When a tool needs external data — consumer complaints, Census demographics — it calls a free federal API directly from your browser. We are not in the middle.

Layer 2: Community tools. Discussions, proposals, needs matching, official evaluations, promise tracking, and civic diagnostics use a lightweight serverless backend (Cloudflare Workers + D1 database). This layer stores community-generated content — posts, votes, diagnostic reports — so neighbors can communicate with each other. It stores no personal data: no names, no emails, no accounts, no passwords. Participation is anonymous. Civic tokens prove you are a community member without revealing who you are. The diagnostic engine produces only deidentified aggregate reports with counts suppressed below 11.

Layer 1 works with zero infrastructure. Layer 2 adds community coordination. Both cost $0 — Cloudflare's free tier handles 100,000 requests per day and 5 million database reads per day. The only paid component is the $7/year domain name.

Why Zero Data Matters

In January 2026, CMS signed an agreement sharing Medicaid data — addresses, birth dates, immigration status, phone numbers for 79 million Americans — with ICE. Families who enrolled in health coverage for their children now face the possibility that enrollment data could be used for enforcement.

CommUnity OS makes surveillance impossible through architecture, not policy. There is no account to create. No login to track. No cookie to follow. No database to subpoena. No analytics to mine. This isn't a privacy promise — it's a physics constraint. You can't hand over data that doesn't exist.

How to Deploy This in Your Community

CommUnity OS is open source. You can deploy it for any community in America in under 5 minutes:

1. Download the zip file (389KB).
2. Go to netlify.com and create a free account.
3. Drag the folder onto the deploy zone.
4. Point your $7 domain at it.
5. Done.

Every tool works for every zip code. The benefit screener covers all 50 states. The wage calculator has all 50 state minimums plus 20 city overrides. The Census, CFPB, EPA, and HMDA data covers the entire country. The only tools that are Chicago-specific are food inspections, building violations, and the 311 tracker — and those can be swapped to any city with a Socrata open data portal.
