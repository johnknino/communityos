Worker Rights

Know what you're owed. Know what's illegal. Know where to report it.

What's the Minimum Wage Where I Work?

⏰ Overtime

If you work more than 40 hours in a week, your employer must pay 1.5x your regular rate for every extra hour. This is federal law (FLSA). Some states require daily overtime after 8 hours. Salaried employees earning under $58,656/year are entitled to overtime as of 2025.

💰 Tips

Your tips are yours. Employers cannot take them. If you earn tips, your employer can pay a lower base wage but your total (base + tips) must equal or exceed the full minimum wage every pay period. If it doesn't, your employer must make up the difference. Tip pooling among servers is legal; sharing tips with managers or owners is not.

🛡️ Retaliation

It is illegal for your employer to fire you, cut your hours, change your schedule, or threaten you for asking about your pay, reporting a safety problem, filing a wage claim, or talking to coworkers about wages. If they do, that's a separate violation you can report.

📋 Pay Stubs

Most states require employers to give you a detailed pay stub showing hours, rate, overtime, deductions, and net pay. If you're not getting one, that's a red flag. In Illinois, employers must provide an itemized statement every pay period.

🚫 Misclassification

If your employer calls you an "independent contractor" but controls your schedule, tells you how to do the work, and provides tools — you may legally be an employee entitled to minimum wage, overtime, and benefits. This is one of the most common forms of wage theft.

How Wage Theft Works

Normal: You work. Your employer pays you at least the legal minimum for every hour, including overtime at 1.5x after 40 hours. Your tips are yours. You get a pay stub.

Broken: Wage theft costs workers an estimated $50 billion per year — more than all robberies, burglaries, and auto thefts combined. Common forms: not paying overtime, paying below minimum wage, stealing tips, misclassifying employees as contractors, making you work off the clock, illegal deductions. Low-wage workers, immigrants, and workers of color are targeted most.

Fix: Document everything — photos of your schedule, pay stubs, hours worked. File a complaint with the DOL Wage and Hour Division (free, regardless of immigration status) or your state labor department. In Illinois, call the Illinois Department of Labor at 312-793-2800. The DOL investigates and can recover back wages. Retaliation for filing is a separate federal violation.
