✓ You can use any benefit you qualify for.

As a U.S. citizen, public charge does not apply to you. Use any benefit your family qualifies for — SNAP, Medicaid, housing, WIC, school lunch, SSI, TANF, and all others. These programs exist for you.

✓ Green card holders are generally safe.

Public charge applies only when you first get your green card or re-enter the US after traveling abroad. Once you are a permanent resident living in the US, using benefits does not put your green card at risk. If you plan to apply for citizenship, benefit use is not part of the naturalization test.

✓ Refugees and asylees are exempt from public charge.

Public charge does NOT apply to refugees, asylees, T visa holders (trafficking victims), U visa holders (crime victims), VAWA self-petitioners, or Special Immigrant Juveniles. Use any benefit you qualify for. This exemption is written into federal law.

Here's what we found:

Important: Under the 2022 public charge rule (8 CFR § 212.22), only these programs count against you: SSI, TANF, state/local cash assistance for income maintenance, and long-term government-funded institutionalization. Everything else — including SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, WIC, school lunch, housing assistance, disaster relief, community health centers, and tax credits — does NOT count. Benefits used by your U.S. citizen children do not affect your case.

This tool provides general information based on the 2022 public charge rule (8 CFR § 212.22) currently in effect. It is not legal advice. Immigration law changes.
