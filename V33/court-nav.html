Eviction Court — What to Expect

In Illinois, a landlord cannot evict you without going through court. They must file a case, serve you papers, and get a judge's order. You have the right to show up and tell your side.

1
You get a summons

The landlord files a case and you receive papers with a court date. Do NOT ignore this. If you don't show up, the judge will likely rule against you automatically (default judgment).

2
Show up on your date

Arrive early. Dress respectfully. You'll check in with the clerk. The courtroom handles many cases at once — expect to wait.

3
You may get a chance to negotiate

Before seeing the judge, many courts offer mediation. You and the landlord can try to agree — more time to move, a payment plan, case dismissal. You do NOT have to agree to anything.

4
The hearing

If you can't agree, the judge hears both sides. Tell the truth. Be brief. Bring your evidence. The judge decides: dismiss the case, give you time, or order eviction. You can appeal.

📎 What to Bring

✓ Your lease (if you have one)
✓ Proof of rent payments (receipts, bank statements, money order stubs)
✓ Photos of any housing problems (mold, no heat, broken locks)
✓ Written communications with your landlord (texts, emails, letters)
✓ The summons/court papers you received
✓ A photo ID
Normal:

Eviction is a last resort after proper notice and due process. The court verifies the landlord followed the law. The tenant has the right to respond, present evidence, and negotiate.

Broken:

Landlords file eviction for retaliation, skip required notices, lock tenants out illegally, or file cases they know will be dismissed just to create an eviction record. Most tenants don't show up — and lose by default. An eviction filing follows you for 7 years even if the case was dismissed.

Fix:

Show up. That alone changes the outcome. Bring evidence. Ask for mediation. If the landlord didn't follow proper notice, tell the judge. If you have an eviction on your record from a dismissed case, Illinois allows record sealing under 735 ILCS 5/9-121. Contact Metropolitan Tenants Organization at 773-292-4988.

Small Claims Court

Small claims court handles disputes up to $10,000 in Illinois. No lawyer needed. You file, pay a small fee, and present your case to a judge. Common cases: security deposit not returned, property damage, unpaid debts, contractor disputes.

1
File a complaint

Go to the clerk's office in the county where the person or business is located. Fill out a complaint form. Pay the filing fee ($50–150 depending on amount). The clerk will give you a court date.

2
Serve the other party

The person you're suing must receive official notice. The clerk can arrange service by sheriff ($30–60). You cannot serve the papers yourself.

3
Prepare your case

Organize your evidence: contracts, receipts, photos, text messages, repair estimates. Write a brief timeline of what happened. Practice explaining it in 3 minutes.

4
Present to the judge

Tell the truth. Be calm. Show your evidence. The judge will ask questions. If the other party doesn't show, you likely win by default. The judge decides at the hearing or within a few days.

📎 What to Bring

✓ All contracts, receipts, invoices
✓ Photos documenting the problem
✓ Text messages, emails, written communications
✓ Repair estimates or replacement costs
✓ A written timeline of events
✓ 3 copies of everything (you, judge, other party)
Normal:

Two people have a dispute over money. Small claims court provides a fast, affordable way to resolve it with a judge — no lawyers needed.

Broken:

People don't know small claims court exists, don't know the dollar limit, get intimidated by the courthouse, or don't know how to collect after they win. Winning the judgment and collecting the money are two different things.

Fix:

File in person at the clerk's office. If you win and the other party doesn't pay, you can garnish wages or bank accounts through the court. In Illinois, the statute of limitations is 5 years for oral contracts, 10 years for written. Court self-help centers can assist with forms.

Traffic Court

Traffic tickets in Chicago and Cook County are handled differently depending on the type. Parking tickets go to Administrative Hearings. Moving violations go to Traffic Court. Red light and speed camera tickets are administrative.

1
Read your ticket carefully

It shows the violation, the fine, the court date (if any), and how to pay or contest. Note the deadline — missing it can double the fine or result in a warrant.

2
Decide: pay or contest

Paying the ticket is an admission. If you contest, you get a hearing. For moving violations, the officer must appear — if they don't, the case is usually dismissed.

3
Request supervision

For minor violations, you can request court supervision instead of a conviction. If you complete the supervision period (usually 3–6 months) without another ticket, it doesn't go on your record.

📎 What to Bring

✓ Your ticket
✓ Driver's license and insurance card
✓ Photos (if the sign was obscured, the light was malfunctioning, etc.)
✓ Proof of repair (if cited for equipment violation)
Normal:

You get a ticket. You either pay or contest it. If you contest, you get a hearing. The system is designed to be navigable without a lawyer.

Broken:

Tickets pile up into debt. Missed payments become warrants. License suspensions make it impossible to get to work. In Chicago, speed camera tickets disproportionately affect Black and Latino neighborhoods. Administrative fines bypass due process protections. Fine amounts are the same regardless of income.

Fix:

If you have outstanding tickets, contact the City of Chicago Department of Finance about payment plans or the Community Service Program. For license reinstatement, call the Illinois Secretary of State Driver Services at 217-782-6212. Check for warrant status before it becomes a bigger problem.

Family Court

Family court handles divorce, custody, child support, orders of protection, and guardianship. These cases are emotional and high-stakes. Knowing the process helps.

1
Orders of protection

If you're in danger, you can get an emergency order of protection the same day — no fee, no lawyer needed. Go to the Domestic Violence courthouse. The order is temporary (14–21 days) until a full hearing. Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233.

2
Custody

Illinois uses "allocation of parental responsibilities" instead of "custody." The court considers the child's best interest, not which parent "wins." You can represent yourself but a lawyer helps significantly. Low-income parents can request a court-appointed attorney in some cases.

3
Child support

Illinois uses an income-shares model — both parents' incomes determine the amount. The Illinois Department of Healthcare and Family Services can help establish, modify, or enforce child support orders at no cost: 1-800-447-4278.

📎 What to Bring

✓ Financial documents (pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements)
✓ Proof of living situation (lease, utility bills)
✓ Documentation of the children's needs (school, medical, childcare costs)
✓ Any existing court orders
✓ Evidence relevant to your case (photos, messages, police reports)
Normal:

Family court resolves disputes about children, safety, and finances when families change. The process is designed to protect children's best interests and ensure both parents contribute.

Broken:

People can't afford lawyers, don't understand the process, miss deadlines that affect their children's lives, or face abusive partners who manipulate the court system. Immigrant parents fear that going to court will expose their status.

Fix:

Family court proceedings are confidential. Immigration status is generally not relevant and should not be raised. For domestic violence: emergency orders are available same-day at no cost. For custody and support: contact Legal Aid Chicago (312-341-1070) or Chicago Volunteer Legal Services (312-332-1624). The Self-Help Center at the Daley Center can help with forms.
